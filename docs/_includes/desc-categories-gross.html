They are the **most effective way** to quickly compare candidates. If you don't have time to watch an entire recording, watch 2-3 rounds of questions in the middle of the meeting. This will take 10-15 minutes. Questions to ask yourself: - Who attended the meeting? Who did not? - Which candidates answered the questions directly? Which ones avoided the question(s) or pivoted to talking points? - Which candidates demonstrated that they understand the complexities of the underlying issues? Which candidates offered overly-simplistic answers? {% elsif include.category == "Questionnaire" %} Questionnaires allow you to compare candidate responses side by side. Often they are published by advocacy groups pushing particular agendas. Questions to ask yourself: - Which candidates responded to the survey? - In what ways did the candidates spin their answers to try and appeal to the organization asking the questions? - Which candidates demonstrated a good understanding of the underlying issues? - To what extent do you agree with the positions of the group running the questionnaire? {% elsif include.category == "EndorsementList" %} Some advocacy groups release endorsements of particular candidates. Look at the advocacy group's positions and philosophies, and see whether they align with your own. Questions to ask yourself: - What political outcomes does this group advocate for? Do you agree with their goals? - On what basis did this group make their endorsement? Did they gather statements from the candidates? - How transparent was their selection process? {% elsif include.category == "ProfileList" %} Candidate profiles allow you to read responses from different candidates side by side.
